5.0 out of 5 starsOne of my all time favorites!
ByAmazon Customeron February 21, 2013
It saddens me to say, but I am much better at writing a negative review than I am a positive one. It is just so hard to pin down exactly why I think a book is great, but all to easy why it is horrible. That being said, this review will probably suck, but this book is awesome.

In a world where super powers are real, the lives of five (six) people are followed as they train to become heroes at the prestigious Lander University. Unlike their classmates, these individuals started as lowly powereds, a group of people who possess amazing abilities but have no control over them. Through a new medical procedure they are able to finally take control of their abilities and level the playing field with the rest of the gifted.

The characters are interesting, developed, relate-able. Normally I am not a big fan of switching view points in a book, but the tales of Hershal/ Roy, Vince, Mary, Nick, and Alice are all so incredibly interesting and well written that the story flows very well. The action is described very well and easy to follow. The characters develop and change, but stay true to their motivations and personalities. They all have different "voices," so it doesn't feel like the same character is speaking through different mouths. The abilities of the different super powereds are well planned out and as logical as super powers can be.

I feel like I am falling far short of what I want to express, but let me sum it up with this: If you love super powers, heroes, action, great characters and story development then buy this book. You won't regret it.

3.0 out of 5 starsEngrossing plot, but has problems
ByGeeky guyon December 28, 2013
Let's get a few things out of the way--other reviewers have complained about the lack of editing and the painful font. Agreed on both points. In particular, good editing would help not only with spelling and grammar, but also suggest adding some variety to the sentence structure.

Other reviewers were happy with the characters. I wasn't. This is a set of people who are supposedly students at a large university, which just happens to have a small, secret, percentage of super powered students in a secret department. All the characters are the type who either take orders or misbehave. None of the students shows much leadership potential. Remember the scene in Men in Black where Will Smith is getting tested along with a group of military types? They, and the characters in this book are all the "best of the best", or at least pretend to be. In MIB, Smith thinks creatively out of the box and solves problems. No Smith types here. Why doesn't anyone wonder why there is such a strict process of elimination? Well, they do get around to this, but not until the book is nearly over. You'd think that these supposedly ambitious, talented, intelligent students would immediately try to figure out the process and work the system. And wouldn't a snobbish, socially adept daughter from an extremely wealthy (worth more than some countries) family say "with whom" rather than "with who"? Maybe that's a grammar problem, or is it a character development problem?

Where this book does well, and is worth the price, is the involved plot. You have to stick with it, and the action could be choreographed more tightly, but there is a decent payoff. Good enough to persuade me to try the next book in the series.

4.0 out of 5 starsA college year Harry Potter novel for Superheroes
ByDana John Haakensenon August 22, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
If you've read any of the reviews, in particular the bad ones, you will know this ebook has some editting issues. The same can be said for many of the books that have been developed because of epublishing. I don't believe for a second that this issue detracts from the overall experience. All of the mistakes are simple to decipher. You might reread a sentence every once and a while, but aside from that it is no big deal.

Another criticism I wanted to address was that this the editing was needed to make the book shorter. I for one am pleased to be spending a realitivly small amount of money on a good fun book that I don't finish inside of a day.

That being said, I really enjoyed this novel. It made me nostalgic for my college days and got me my Super-fix. While some of the "college interactions" such as the partying and the sex might seem a bit cliched, I find that there is enough variety in the characters to make it feel realistic. There was real if somewhat predictable character growth that changes how you feel about some characters. There is even a bit of mystery that isn't resolved in this novel that leaves you hungry for more in Year 2. The snarkiness of alot of the characters reminds me alot of my college experience as well.

Also the comparison I make to Harry Potter is that it's about a group of people learning to use their powers better. This is NOT A CHILDREN'S BOOK. There is alot of swearing and sexual content.

I'm in my twenties and enjoyed it a great deal. I can't wait for Year 2!

3.0 out of 5 starsSolid fun (for certain values of fun)
ByJ Son December 20, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
I'm terrible at talking about what I *like* about a book. What works for me depends a lot on what I'm in the mood to be reading. I am usually much more clear about what I don't like, so I want to preface this review with two statements:

1. Yes, there are still plenty of proofreading errors throughout the book. (As of when I picked it up in late November 2013.)

2. I checked the book out for free as a Prime member, and then bought it and the sequel.

And, I think that sums up my feelings about the book pretty concisely if you don't want to read further.

Knowing that this book started as an episodic web enterprise goes a long way to explaining what I consider the two biggest problems with the story: the pacing is poor and the writing is not as lean as it might otherwise be. This *feels* like an outstanding second draft of a NaNoWriMo project that needed one or two more go-rounds with the editing pen to trim some of the scenes that don't add substantially to the overall plot.

One of the positives of the episodic writing is that you spend a lot of time with the characters. The downside is that there's little economy of phrasing. Combat scenes take up multiple chapters, and it feels like watching Yu Gi Oh (or, for the older readers, the current season of How I Met Your Mother): lots of words, little forward motion of the story.

In addition, the stakes don't exist until you get to the very last 10-15% of the story when something occurs that had no foreshadowing at all. I believe the tension of discovery of what makes the kids "special" is supposed to be there, but I never felt it. SpoilerNotSpoiler: when the outing occurs, it's not even the biggest news on the page.

The story was a slog to get into. I had no idea why it was taking so long for things to happen. I don't know what kept me going throughout the book except that at some point, I acknowledged that I had nothing else I wanted to read, and perhaps the story just needed to unfold over the entire four books instead of this one. By the time I finished it, I was interested enough to pony up the cash and buy my own copy (despite the fact that I could keep it as long as I like as a Prime member, and the book is online for free anyway).

I'm hoping that Hayes has a plan that will ultimately satisfy.

4.0 out of 5 starsTwisted look at college for superhumans
ByMvarguson May 23, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
Drew Hayes came up with a new twist to the "superkid going to school" tale and tried to spin out a new tale. He does a rather good job of stretching some of the clichés in this genre and built up some solid characters. out of the newer superhero tales Ive read, this is definitely the best of the group.

Of course, this does not mean it's up to the level of "Wearing the Cape" or "Velveteen v the Junior Super Patriots", but it is still a solid (if extremely long) tale.

My only real complaint would be that I found a couple of the characters to be completely unsufferable. However, its clear that Drew meant them to be that way. I have to admit that he's one of the few newer authors I've read who actually can write an almost anti-hero without having me want to throw the book away every time they are in a scene. that doesn't mean I like the character (there are 2 in this story I'd love to see dropped into a pool of hydrochloric acid", but I can read scenes with them as stars without my hand twitching to keep me from throwing my kindle at the nearest wall.

The biggest problem here is that this book is "year 1" of what might someday be 4 years of college. The tale doesn't end, but merely finds a convenient point to spin to a halt. If you want a complete story, do not pick up this book.

Solid writing though, I definitely can recommend this to others.

2.0 out of 5 starsDoes no one use a proofreader anymore?
ByHPEon May 30, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
Overall, the story was interesting. HOWEVER, this book is in dire need of an editor to trim things down (just because a story *can* be over 800 pages long does not mean it *needs* to be over 800 pages long) and at least two proofreaders (one to correct the spelling/grammar errors and misused words, and another to catch all the *missing words*. I would have given it 4 stars, but one star removed for basic spelling/grammar issues, and another star removed for just randomly leaving out words in sentences.

The biggest issue is the sheer amount of missing words in the Kindle edition. As a reader one cannot know whether this was due to the author just not writing the words down or whether it was a printing/typesetting error by whomever put the text into the printed version. The following is by no means a comprehensive list. There are many more errors in the book than these. If things like this will not bother you or confuse you, then you might be able to get through it. I should have heeded the other reviews that commented on the same issues.

If the author/publisher can fix these issues in the current book and make sure these issues are addressed in the upcoming books in the series, then the series does have the promise of being a fun entry into the YA/super-hero genre.

Examples (missing words in all caps):
location 4327 - "We have it, even IF it isn't reigned in.."
5856 - "was the knowledge that THAT was only a sixth of..."
7632 - "inform Mrs. Shaw of WHAT she has forgotten"
8155 - "Roy will tell me all about WHAT he's been UP TO at length tomorrow"
8302 - "and in it there will BE tons of things..."
8867 - "adjusted the angle THAT Hershel's shirt..."
(there are many more examples)

Then there's words that have been used that are just flat out wrong (correct words in all caps):
822 - "word" is not the same as "Word" (because it's in reference to a brand-name piece of software)
2288 - "must" does not mean MUCH
2531 - "That" does not mean THEY
4060 - "growing" does not mean GREW (present tense vs. past tense)
5776 - "to" does not mean YOU
5928 - "imagine" does not mean IMAGE
8869 - "delivery" is not the same as DELIVER
9376 - "peaked" is not the same as PIQUED
11349 - "verdant orange" um, what? I do not think this means what you think it means. This would be like saying GREEN-ORANGE
11435 - there is no piece of clothing called a "t-short", but there is a T-SHIRT
11951 - "sliver" does not mean SILVER
12188 - "clam" does not mean CALM
12307 - "stuck" does not mean STRUCK
12795 - "particularly" is not the same as PARTICULAR
(again, many more examples of this throughout the book)

5.0 out of 5 starsMost addicting read I have had in a long time!
ByKindle Customeron March 5, 2016
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
I have to say how much I have been loving this series. I read a ton of great books but this series has been "sneak extra reading time at work" addicting like I haven't experienced for a long time. Interesting worldbuilding where people with Super Powers exist and a few make it through the training be official Heroes. If I had to compare it would be a cross between Harry Potter or another school based world (which I love) and Buffy the Vampire Slayer as it has that smart, funny side with an ensemble. Very well written with great characters. The books are long and thus allow for a lot of character building and day to day school activiities without skipping right to the plot moving points, there are a lot of interesting plot threads due to mutiple well differentiated characters so I never felt it was Robert Jordan tedious.
